SR-22 Insurance Declaration Page Example & Guide

Home / Blog / Blog Details

Navigating the world of auto insurance can be tricky, especially when you’re dealing with an SR-22 filing. Whether you’ve been convicted of a DUI, caught driving without insurance, or accumulated too many traffic violations, an SR-22 is often required to prove financial responsibility. But what exactly does an SR-22 Insurance Declaration Page look like, and how does it work in today’s fast-changing regulatory landscape?

Understanding the SR-22 Form

An SR-22 is not insurance—it’s a certificate filed by your insurer to confirm you carry the minimum required liability coverage. The SR-22 Insurance Declaration Page is a critical document that outlines your policy details, ensuring compliance with state laws.

Why You Might Need an SR-22

  • DUI/DWI convictions – Many states mandate an SR-22 after alcohol-related offenses.
  • Driving without insurance – If caught uninsured, you may need an SR-22 to reinstate your license.
  • Repeated traffic violations – Excessive tickets or at-fault accidents can trigger this requirement.
  • License suspension or revocation – Some states require an SR-22 before reinstatement.

What’s on an SR-22 Insurance Declaration Page?

Your SR-22 Insurance Declaration Page will include key details such as:

1. Policyholder Information

  • Full name and address
  • Driver’s license number
  • Policy number and effective dates

2. Vehicle Details

  • Year, make, and model of insured vehicles
  • VIN (Vehicle Identification Number)

3. Coverage Limits

  • State-mandated minimum liability coverage (e.g., 25/50/25 in many states)
  • Additional coverages like collision or comprehensive (if applicable)

4. Insurance Company Information

  • Name and contact details of the insurer
  • SR-22 filing number

5. State-Specific Requirements

  • Some states have unique SR-22 rules (e.g., Virginia uses an FR-44 for high-risk drivers).

How to Get an SR-22 Insurance Declaration Page

Step 1: Contact Your Insurance Provider

Not all insurers offer SR-22 filings. If yours doesn’t, you may need to switch to a high-risk auto insurance provider.

Step 2: Pay the Filing Fee

Insurers typically charge a one-time fee (usually $15-$50) to file an SR-22 with the state.

Step 3: Maintain Continuous Coverage

If your policy lapses, your insurer must notify the state, which could lead to license suspension.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Letting your policy lapse – Even a one-day gap can restart the SR-22 clock.
  • Underestimating costs – High-risk drivers often pay 2-3x more for insurance.
  • Assuming all insurers file SR-22s – Smaller providers may not offer this service.

The Future of SR-22 Filings

With rising insurance fraud and stricter DUI laws, some states are moving toward electronic SR-22 filings, reducing paperwork. Additionally, usage-based insurance (UBI) programs may help high-risk drivers lower premiums over time by proving safe driving habits.

If you’re dealing with an SR-22 requirement, staying informed and maintaining compliance is crucial. Always review your SR-22 Insurance Declaration Page carefully and consult with your insurer or DMV if you have questions.
