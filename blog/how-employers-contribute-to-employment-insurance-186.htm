How Employers Contribute to Employment Insurance

Home / Blog / Blog Details

Employment insurance (EI) is a critical safety net for workers facing job loss, illness, or other disruptions. While employees often focus on their own contributions, employers play an equally vital role in funding and shaping these systems. In today’s rapidly changing job market—marked by automation, gig work, and economic uncertainty—understanding how employers contribute to EI is more important than ever.

The Employer’s Role in Funding Employment Insurance

Employers are legally required to contribute to employment insurance programs in most countries. These contributions are typically calculated as a percentage of an employee’s wages, matched or supplemented by the employer’s share.

How Employer Contributions Are Calculated

In the U.S., for example, employers pay a federal unemployment tax (FUTA) of 6% on the first $7,000 of each employee’s wages. Many states also require additional unemployment insurance taxes. Similarly, in Canada, employers contribute 1.4 times the employee’s EI premium. These rates can vary based on factors like:
- Industry risk levels
- Company size
- Claims history

Why Employer Contributions Matter

Without employer contributions, EI systems would struggle to remain solvent. Employers essentially share the financial burden of workforce instability, ensuring that laid-off workers can still access benefits while searching for new jobs.

How Employers Influence EI Policies

Beyond just paying into the system, employers often have a say in how EI programs are structured. Lobbying groups and industry associations frequently advocate for changes to EI policies, such as:
- Adjusting premium rates
- Expanding or restricting eligibility
- Modifying benefit durations

The Gig Economy Challenge

One of the biggest debates today revolves around gig workers and independent contractors. Traditional EI systems were designed for full-time employees, leaving many gig workers without coverage. Some employers (like Uber and Lyft) have pushed back against reclassifying gig workers as employees, while others argue for reforms to include them in EI.

Employer Best Practices for Managing EI Costs

Smart employers don’t just pay into EI—they actively manage their contributions to minimize costs while supporting their workforce.

Reducing Layoffs Through Retention Strategies

High turnover can lead to higher EI premiums. Employers can mitigate this by:
- Offering training and upskilling programs
- Implementing flexible work arrangements
- Improving workplace conditions to reduce voluntary quits

Navigating Economic Downturns

During recessions, some governments offer EI premium relief or subsidies to help employers retain staff. Companies that proactively engage with these programs can reduce financial strain while keeping skilled workers.

The Future of Employer EI Contributions

As labor markets evolve, so too will the role of employers in EI systems. Key trends to watch include:
- Automation and job displacement – Will employers pay higher premiums if robots replace workers?
- Universal basic income (UBI) experiments – Could UBI reduce reliance on traditional EI?
- Global remote work – How will cross-border employment affect EI contributions?

Employers who stay ahead of these changes will be better positioned to attract talent and maintain financial stability.

By understanding and engaging with employment insurance systems, employers don’t just fulfill a legal obligation—they contribute to a more resilient workforce and economy.
